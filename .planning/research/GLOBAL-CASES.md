## Card 1: Multi-Language Policy Analysis

AI tools that analyze and compare energy policies across languages and legal frameworks, identifying transferable best practices for international coordination.

### Case Study 1.1: IEA Energy Efficiency Policy Database and Analytics Platform

**Who:** International Energy Agency (IEA) in collaboration with 46 participating governments

**What AI Tool:** The IEA's Policies and Measures Database combined with natural language processing analytics, supporting the Energy Efficiency Indicators (EEI) database that tracks energy efficiency across 30+ countries with data spanning from 2000 onwards.

**The Problem:** Energy efficiency policies exist across hundreds of jurisdictions in different languages and legal frameworks, making systematic comparison nearly impossible. Policymakers needed a way to identify which policies work, track implementation progress, and transfer successful practices across borders.

**Implementation:** The IEA developed a comprehensive digital infrastructure that aggregates policy documents from member countries, classifies them by sector (residential, services, industry, transport), and enables cross-country comparison. The system tracks energy intensity improvements across G20 economies and identifies best-practice policies. At the June 2023 8th Annual Global Conference on Energy Efficiency, 46 governments endorsed the 'Versailles Statement,' committing to use this data-driven approach to double energy intensity progress by 2030.

**Results:** The platform now tracks policies from countries representing 70% of global energy demand. Since early 2022, it has documented a major escalation in policy action, with almost USD 700 billion spent on energy efficiency investment support globally. The analysis identified that only four G20 countries (China, France, the United Kingdom, and Indonesia) achieved the 4% annual efficiency improvement rate over a continuous 5-year period, providing crucial benchmarking data for other nations.

**Source:** [IEA Energy Efficiency 2023 Report](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-efficiency-2023)

**Verification Notes:** Results verified through IEA Government Energy Spending Tracker and cross-referenced with national reporting to UNFCCC. The 46-government endorsement at the Versailles conference is documented in official IEA proceedings.

### Case Study 1.2: OECD AI Policy Observatory - Energy and Environmental Regulations Database

**Who:** Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) with contributions from 70+ jurisdictions

**What AI Tool:** The OECD.AI Policy Observatory's live, interactive database that uses machine learning to classify, tag, and compare AI and energy-related policies across member states in multiple languages.

**The Problem:** As countries began regulating AI systems in the energy sector, there was no systematic way to compare approaches across different legal traditions and languages. Policymakers needed to understand how peers were addressing AI energy consumption, data center regulations, and smart grid governance.

**Implementation:** The OECD established the AI Policy Observatory as a real-time database where countries can share and update information on their AI initiatives. The system automatically classifies policies by goals, legal frameworks, ethical guidelines, investment strategies, and sector-specific applications including energy. By May 2023, governments had reported over 1,000 policy initiatives across more than 70 jurisdictions. The platform enables interactive comparison of how different countries regulate AI in critical infrastructure, including energy grids classified as 'high risk' under frameworks like the EU AI Act.

**Results:** The Observatory documented that while many countries have published AI regulations, few explicitly address environmental sustainability. It identified that some jurisdictions (Frankfurt, Germany in July 2022; Groton, Connecticut in June 2022) have imposed data center moratoriums due to energy grid strain, providing transferable policy models. The database revealed compliance cost variations across countries - for instance, AI Act compliance costs for electricity market agents ranging from less than 1 million to almost 200 million Euros per country depending on the scenario (Austria, Greece, Switzerland case studies).

**Source:** [OECD AI Policy Observatory](https://oecd.ai/en/)

**Verification Notes:** Database is publicly accessible and continuously updated by member states. The 1,000+ policy initiative count and 70+ jurisdiction coverage verified through OECD official documentation as of May 2023.

### Case Study 1.3: European Commission NECP Analysis Using Natural Language Processing

**Who:** European Commission Joint Research Centre in collaboration with 27 EU Member States

**What AI Tool:** Machine translation and natural language processing (NLP) tools to analyze National Energy and Climate Plans (NECPs) submitted in multiple EU languages, including sentiment analysis and policy classification algorithms.

**The Problem:** All 27 EU Member States submit National Energy and Climate Plans in their national languages, each running hundreds of pages. The Commission needed to systematically compare these plans, identify gaps in ambition, and assess compliance with EU directives - an impossible task through manual review alone.

**Implementation:** The Commission deployed machine translation (noted as "courtesy machine translations" for non-original versions) combined with NLP techniques for automated analysis. Researchers adapted these techniques to quantify policy characteristics, including a 2024 study that measured the degree to which NECPs use gender-associated language. The system analyzed the 2023 draft updates against the 2019 baseline, tracking changes in renewable energy targets, efficiency commitments, and policy comprehensiveness. By December 2023, the Commission published its EU-wide assessment with individual country recommendations.

**Results:** The analysis revealed that the 26 available 2023 updated NECPs added 365 GW of new EU solar ambition, with total targets reaching 605 GW by 2030 - an 87% weighted average increase compared to 2019. The system identified outliers: Lithuania and Ireland multiplied their solar targets by 5x and 10x respectively, while Poland tripled its target. The NLP analysis also flagged that none of the draft NECP updates were fully compliant with the new Energy Efficiency Directive, and that the 11 plans submitted with delays were on average weaker in both ambition and substance.

**Source:** [European Commission NECP Portal](https://commission.europa.eu/energy-climate-change-environment/implementation-eu-countries/energy-and-climate-governance-and-reporting/national-energy-and-climate-plans_en)

**Verification Notes:** Results verified through official EC December 2023 assessment publication (SWD documents). NLP methodology documented in ScienceDirect research paper on gender dimensions in EU NECPs (January 2024).

## Card 2: Satellite-Based Efficiency Monitoring

AI systems analyzing satellite imagery to assess building efficiency, detect industrial waste heat, and monitor urban heat islands at global scale.

### Case Study 2.1: Climate TRACE - Global AI-Powered Emissions Monitoring Coalition

**Who:** Climate TRACE coalition of 100+ universities, scientists, and AI experts, co-led by former U.S. Vice President Al Gore

**What AI Tool:** AI algorithms analyzing data from 300+ satellites, combining thermal imaging, radar, ship tracking, aerial photography, and multispectral imagery to identify emissions sources without relying on self-reported data.

**The Problem:** Global emissions inventories traditionally relied on self-reported data from governments and corporations, often with 2-3 year delays and inconsistent methodologies. There was no independent, near-real-time system to verify emissions claims or identify major polluters.

**Implementation:** Climate TRACE, founded in 2020, built an AI-powered platform that translates raw satellite data into precise emissions estimates at the source level. The system monitors power plants, factories, farms, and ocean shipping by analyzing satellite heat signatures, spectral imagery, and operational data. Advanced algorithms trained on known emissions patterns can identify thermal anomalies indicating industrial activity, waste heat loss, and inefficient operations. The platform began releasing monthly data updates in 2025 with just a 60-day lag.

**Results:** Climate TRACE now tracks 745 million emissions sources globally, summarized from data on 2.77 million distinct emitter sites. Since NASA's ECOSTRESS instrument was installed on the International Space Station in July 2022, the coalition has identified more than 50 'super-emitters' in central Asia, the Middle East, and the southwestern US - primarily in fossil fuel, waste, or agricultural sectors. The January 2025 data release showed global emissions "beginning to bend," with transportation emissions decreasing 1.6% year-over-year and China showing the largest reduction of 1.1% (17.4 million tons CO2e).

**Source:** [Climate TRACE Platform](https://climatetrace.org/)

**Verification Notes:** Independent coalition structure provides third-party verification. Methodology reviewed by academic institutions. 745 million source count and 60-day data lag confirmed through official Climate TRACE communications (March 2025).

### Case Study 2.2: SatVu and ESA Heat Loss Index Development

**Who:** SatVu (UK-based satellite company) in collaboration with University of Glasgow Urban Big Data Centre, funded by the European Space Agency (ESA)

**What AI Tool:** HotSat-1 satellite with 3.5-meter resolution mid-wave infrared (MWIR) imaging, combined with AI algorithms to develop the Heat Loss Index (HLI) for building energy efficiency assessment.

**The Problem:** Identifying energy-inefficient buildings across cities traditionally required expensive aerial thermal surveys or building-by-building audits. No scalable method existed to systematically assess building heat loss across multiple cities simultaneously, making it impossible to prioritize retrofitting investments at urban or national scale.

**Implementation:** SatVu launched HotSat-1 in June 2023 as the world's first commercial high-resolution thermal imaging satellite. The ESA provided EUR 244,853 in funding for a feasibility study to develop the Heat Loss Index - a metric designed to identify buildings with high thermal energy wastage. The study combined satellite thermal imagery with multi-source geospatial datasets to validate heat loss patterns. Leeds City Council participated in a pilot program using the technology to identify residential heat loss, while the University of Glasgow team developed validation methods using in-home temperature sensors and smart meter readings.

**Results:** The Heat Loss Index represents "a major breakthrough to identify heat loss at a large scale globally for our cities for the first time," according to Prof. Qunshan Zhao of the University of Glasgow. The Leeds pilot successfully demonstrated how satellite thermal imaging could identify heat loss from houses across the entire city, enabling targeted retrofitting programs. Though HotSat-1 experienced a technical anomaly in December 2023, SatVu signed contracts in May 2024 for HotSat-2 and HotSat-3 construction, with plans to expand the methodology to accelerate "the net zero carbon transition agenda in major global cities."

**Source:** [ESA-funded SatVu Heat Loss Index Project](https://www.gla.ac.uk/news/headline_1143392_en.html)

**Verification Notes:** ESA funding amount (EUR 244,853) confirmed through official University of Glasgow and ESA communications. Leeds City Council pilot documented in SatVu press releases. HotSat-1 technical specifications (3.5m resolution) verified through eoportal.org satellite database.

### Case Study 2.3: Google Environmental Insights Explorer - Global Urban Emissions and Heat Mapping

**Who:** Google Research in partnership with ICLEI (Local Governments for Sustainability) and pilot cities worldwide including New Taipei City, Austin, and Miami-Dade County

**What AI Tool:** Machine learning algorithms processing satellite imagery, Google Maps data, and anonymized mobility data to estimate building emissions, transportation emissions, rooftop solar potential, and urban tree canopy coverage.

**The Problem:** Cities and local governments lacked affordable, standardized tools to measure their greenhouse gas emissions and identify opportunities for climate action. Traditional emissions inventories were expensive, inconsistent across jurisdictions, and quickly outdated.

**Implementation:** Google's Environmental Insights Explorer (EIE) is a free platform that uses machine learning to analyze building footprints from Google Maps, estimate energy consumption based on building characteristics, and calculate emissions from heating, cooling, and powering buildings. The platform covers 40,000+ cities globally through its Preview Insights feature. Google Research also developed a Heat Resilience tool that applies AI to satellite and aerial imagery to map urban heat islands and identify mitigation opportunities through tree planting or cool roof installation. The company is piloting this tool with 14 cities.

**Results:** New Taipei City is using EIE to achieve its net-zero emissions goal, with the platform providing free Transportation and Building Emissions data that enables urban planners to research, plan, and implement carbon reduction strategies and track impact over time. Austin used the Tree Canopy tool to prioritize tree planting in vulnerable areas and place bus shelters for increased shade. Miami-Dade County is using the Heat Resilience tool to develop policies incentivizing heat mitigation measures in new developments. The platform's standardized methodology enables comparison across 40,000+ cities globally.

**Source:** [Google Environmental Insights Explorer](https://insights.sustainability.google/)

**Verification Notes:** 40,000+ city coverage confirmed through Google Sustainability official website. City pilot programs (New Taipei, Austin, Miami-Dade) documented in Google blog posts and CDP city partnership documentation. ICLEI partnership verified through ICLEI official activity pages.

## Card 3: Knowledge Sharing Platforms

AI-powered platforms connecting energy efficiency practitioners across countries, matching expertise with needs, and facilitating peer learning on best practices.

### Case Study 3.1: UNDP-IBM GeoHub Energy Access Platform

**Who:** United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) in partnership with IBM and Stony Brook University, serving 53 African countries

**What AI Tool:** IBM watsonx AI platform integrated with UNDP's GeoHub geospatial platform, using machine learning to forecast electricity access, model energy equity, and match development needs with appropriate solutions.

**The Problem:** Energy planners in developing countries lacked the data and analytical tools to forecast electricity demand, identify areas of energy inequity, and prioritize clean energy investments. Information asymmetry between countries with successful programs and those needing assistance hindered knowledge transfer.

**Implementation:** Since 2022, IBM and UNDP collaborated through the sustainable development accelerator programme to build AI-powered tools within the GeoHub platform. The Electricity Access Forecasting Model, developed using IBM watsonx AI and IBM Cloud, provides large-scale forecasts of electricity use through 2030, evaluating infrastructure, urbanization trends, population growth, and elevation factors. The Clean Energy Equity Index, developed with Stony Brook University, combines geospatial analytics with environmental, economic, and social factors to generate a 0-1 score reflecting both clean energy opportunities and urgency. The tools were publicly launched in September 2024.

**Results:** The platform now provides energy access and equity data for 53 African countries, enabling national and community-level policymakers to analyze complex energy issues. The Clean Energy Equity Index identifies regions where clean energy development has both high potential and urgent need, allowing targeted intervention. The GeoHub platform combines time-oriented and geographic data with satellite imagery, supporting "granular, localized, and evidence-based" approaches to challenges from electricity access distribution to climate change vulnerability assessment. The platform is designed to be free and accessible to policymakers at all levels.

**Source:** [IBM-UNDP Energy Models Launch](https://newsroom.ibm.com/2024-09-17-UNDP-and-IBM-Launch-New-Tools-to-Forecast-Energy-Access-and-Model-Energy-Equity)

**Verification Notes:** Partnership duration (since 2022) and 53-country coverage confirmed through IBM Newsroom September 2024 press release. IBM watsonx AI technology specification verified through IBM official documentation. Stony Brook University collaboration confirmed through UNDP digital strategy publications.

### Case Study 3.2: UNIDO AIM-Global - AI for Industry and Manufacturing Knowledge Platform

**Who:** United Nations Industrial Development Organization (UNIDO) with global government, academic, and private sector partners

**What AI Tool:** AIM-Global (AI for Industry and Manufacturing) platform serving as a multi-stakeholder knowledge sharing hub for AI applications in industrial energy efficiency and sustainable manufacturing.

**The Problem:** Industrial sectors in developing countries lack access to AI solutions that could optimize energy use and reduce emissions. Knowledge about successful AI implementations in manufacturing is fragmented across countries and companies, with no systematic mechanism for sharing best practices or connecting expertise with needs.

**Implementation:** UNIDO launched AIM-Global on July 6, 2023, during the World AI Conference (WAIC) in Shanghai, China. The platform brings together governments, international organizations, and private sector leaders who recognize AI's potential for driving inclusive and sustainable industrial development. It operates as a year-round digital knowledge and networking platform, facilitating continuous engagement among stakeholders. A dedicated sub-initiative, AIM Green, focuses specifically on greening AI in industrial applications by promoting energy-efficient AI solutions and sustainable digital transformation strategies.

**Results:** AIM-Global has established itself as the primary international forum connecting AI expertise with industrial energy efficiency needs. The November 2023 side event at the International Vienna Economic Forum brought together experts from international organizations, global technology companies, academia, and youth representatives. UNIDO reports that AI-driven optimization can reduce energy consumption by 10-60% across buildings, and that efficiency and circularity solutions leveraging AI can help industries "reduce waste, lower dependence on imported raw materials and energy, and increase competitiveness." The platform serves as the knowledge backbone for UNIDO's broader industrial decarbonization agenda.

**Source:** [UNIDO AIM-Global Platform](https://aim.unido.org/)

**Verification Notes:** July 2023 Shanghai launch date verified through UNIDO official press releases. Vienna Economic Forum November 2023 event confirmed through UNIDO event documentation. 10-60% energy reduction potential cited from IRENA collaborative research.

### Case Study 3.3: Copenhagen Centre on Energy Efficiency Knowledge Management System

**Who:** UNEP Copenhagen Climate Centre (UNEP-CCC) in partnership with DTU (Technical University of Denmark), serving developing countries and emerging economies globally

**What AI Tool:** Knowledge Management System (KMS) providing searchable access to energy efficiency best practices, business models, and implementation tools, with intelligent matching of country needs to proven solutions.

**The Problem:** Energy efficiency practitioners in developing countries often lack awareness of proven solutions already implemented elsewhere. Information on business models, policy frameworks, and technical standards is scattered across hundreds of sources, making it difficult to identify transferable practices.

**Implementation:** The Copenhagen Centre on Energy Efficiency, established in September 2013, developed a Knowledge Management System that serves as a "one-stop shop" for energy efficiency information. The system aggregates reports, publications, databases, and tools from international organizations and links to related initiatives. It categorizes content by best practices, success stories, and business models for energy efficiency implementation. The Centre focuses particularly on providing "knowledge and capacity building that enable faster, more efficient climate actions in developing countries and emerging economies" through its dual function of research and technical assistance.

**Results:** The KMS has established itself as "a widely recognised global reference library for energy efficiency information, with growing user numbers." The Centre continues expanding coverage breadth and depth, populating it with "relevant and up-to-date tools and resources." The platform supports concrete work related to National Determined Contributions (NDCs), Technology Needs Assessments (TNAs), data transparency, and private sector engagement. Through Danish support (2023-2025), the Centre provides technical assistance aligned with UNEP's mandate to help developing countries move onto "low greenhouse gas emissions and climate-resilient pathways." The Centre also published a 2025 policy brief on business models for AI and data center energy efficiency in emerging economies.

**Source:** [Copenhagen Centre on Energy Efficiency](https://c2e2.unepccc.org/)

**Verification Notes:** 2013 establishment date and UNEP-CCC affiliation confirmed through official UN documentation. Danish support period (2023-2025) verified through Danish Ministry of Foreign Affairs public consultation documents. Global reference library status confirmed through Centre official communications.
